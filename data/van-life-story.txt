The Magnificent Odyssey of Derek from the Van: A Totally Real and Not At All Exaggerated Tale of Remote Work in America's Finest City

Chapter 1: In Which Derek Acquires the Van and Immediately Regrets It (But Only a Little)

My name is Derek. Derek from the Van. That's what they call me now, ever since I moved into a 2004 Dodge Sprinter with 187,000 miles on the clock and a check engine light that had been on so long it felt like a roommate. I didn't set out to become Derek from the Van. I set out to become Derek from the Luxury Apartment with a Rooftop Deck and a Kombucha on Tap. But then the tech layoffs hit, my Spotify premium got converted to the ad-supported version, and suddenly the math on a $2,400-a-month one-bedroom in North Park started to feel like I was funding a small nation's military budget.

So I bought a van. On Craigslist. From a guy named "SurfSeth420" who definitely had more followers on TikTok than he had functioning brake pads. The van was called "The Biscuit" because, according to SurfSeth, "dude, it's like, biscuit-shaped, man." He was not wrong. The Biscuit had the aerodynamic profile of a sleeping refrigerator. But she was mine, and she ran, and she had a aux cord that still worked if you held the cable at a 37-degree angle and believed in yourself.

I drove The Biscuit to San Diego for one reason and one reason only: the weather. When you're living in a van, weather is not a small talk topic. Weather is existential. You are, quite literally, outside with whatever nature decides to throw at you. And San Diego said, "Derek, I got you, bro," with 262 days of sunshine a year and temperatures that never quite committed to being either hot or cold. It was weather that couldn't make up its mind, just like me about whether this lifestyle was a revolutionary act of freedom or a cry for help.

I parked The Biscuit in a Costco parking lot in Chula Vista, because SurfSeth420 had told me this was "tactically optimal," and I didn't know what that meant but it sounded serious. I cracked a Truly Tropical at 4:30 PM because I had earned it, and I opened my laptop. I was, at that moment, a remote worker. A remote worker in a van. In a Costco parking lot. With 187,000 miles on the clock and a check engine light that was basically the third roommate I never asked for.

This is the story of how I, Derek from the Van, accidentally became a folk hero of the San Diego gig economy, had a brief and ill-advised affair with a seagull, got hired by a startup that thought "disruption" was a personality trait, lost my laptop in the ocean, found it again, got banned from a coffee shop for reasons that are still not clear to me, and ultimately figured out that the real treasure was the ramen I ate out of the van's sink at 2 AM while watching the stars.

No wait. The real treasure was the friends I made along the way. The ramen was a close second, though. Ramen is always a close second.

Chapter 2: The Great Desk Dilemma (Or: Why Your Dining Table Is Now Your Office)

The first challenge of van life, before you even get to the Instagram part, is desk height. Let me explain something about vans. Vans are designed for hauling things. Cargo. Sports equipment. Large quantities of the same condiment you bought at Costco that you will never finish in your lifetime. Vans are not designed for sitting in a folding chair with a laptop on your lap while pretending you're having a productive morning. Your lap is not a desk. Your lap is a lap. Your lap has been a lap for your entire life and it has never once asked to be promoted to desk duty.

I tried the folding chair approach for exactly three days. Three days in, my lower back filed a formal grievance with HR (me), and HR (also me) said, "Yeah, that's fair, we're going to need to make some structural changes." I bought a collapsible camping table from REI. It cost $39.95 and it was the best $39.95 I ever spent, except for the $4.99 I spent on a tube of chapstick in 2019 that genuinely changed my life. The camping table sat at a perfect 28 inches high, which is apparently the exact height at which a human being can type without looking like a goblin hunched over a fire. The goblin energy was still there — I was, after all, a guy in a van — but the back pain retreated to a dull roar.

The real innovation came when I discovered I could Velcro my laptop to a piece of plywood and mount said plywood to the wall using a system of bungee cords and prayers. I called it The Command Center. It looked like a hostage situation had been set up inside The Biscuit, but it worked. I could sit on my $29.99 memory foam camping pad (on sale from $49.99, which means I basically made money) and have my laptop at eye level. I looked like a Bond villain reviewing missile launch codes. In reality, I was reviewing quarterly SaaS metrics for a startup that made an app that helped people find the exact right time to post things on Instagram. I am not making this up. This was my job. I was helping influencers optimize their posting schedules from inside a van named after a breakfast bread product. The algorithm, as it turns out, does not care about your emotional journey.

Chapter 3: The Coffee Shop Chronicles (Or: How I Got Banned from Modern Times Cafe and What It Taught Me About Freedom)

San Diego has coffee shops. A lot of coffee shops. Like, an irresponsible number of coffee shops. You cannot throw a avocado toast without hitting seven independent roasters, each of which has a waiting list for their cardamom oat milk cold brew that is somehow both extremely long and extremely casual. You wait in line for 40 minutes and when you get to the front, the barista says, "Hey, what brings you in today?" and you say, "Coffee," and they say, "I love that," and you both pretend that this exchange was meaningful.

I had a routine. I would wake up at 6:47 AM — never 6:30, because 6:30 is for people with their lives in order — and I would walk to Modern Times Cafe in North Park, which had the best coffee and the worst wifi in the known universe. The wifi was so bad that it once took me 45 minutes to send a two-word email. The email said "k, thanks." It was a masterpiece of efficiency born of necessity. But the coffee. Oh, the coffee. It was dark and smoky and it tasted like someone had taken the concept of a robust red wine and said, "What if we did that but also woke you up?" I was in love. I was in love with a cappuccino. At 6:47 in the morning. In a coffee shop. I was becoming one of those people.

Here's what happened with the banning. I don't entirely understand it myself, to be honest. I was three hours into a Zoom call with my team — seven people on a video call, all pretending to be paying attention while actually doom-scrolling LinkedIn and wondering if this meeting could have been an email — and I had my phone on speaker next to my laptop, which was normal, and I was also simultaneously listening to a podcast through one earbud, which was also normal, and I was eating a breakfast burrito from the place next door, which was ALSO NORMAL, and at some point I started narrating the podcast out loud? Not to anyone in particular. Just. Out loud. To the room. Because I was thinking about what the host said and I wanted to react to it in real time and my internal narrator had no volume control. And the barista — her name was Jade, we were FRIENDS, or so I thought — Jade came over and said, "Hey Derek, a lot of people are saying..." and I said, "Jade, I'm on a call, can it wait?" and she said, "Derek, you've been narrating a podcast about the history of the potato chip for 20 minutes and several customers have left," and I said, "That's because it's FASCINATING, Jade," and she said, "You're banned," and I said, "From what?" and she said, "From here. You're banned from Modern Times. Forever. I'm sorry. It's not me, it's the customers."

I was devastated. I was humiliated. I was also, if I'm being honest, a little bit validated, because it takes a special kind of energy to get banned from a place that prides itself on being extremely chill. Modern Times Cafe had drawings of cats on the walls. You could bring your dog inside. They had a lending library of books that people just... left. And they banned me for being too enthusiastic about podcasts. I wore that ban like a badge of honor. I got a sticker that said "I GOT BANNED FROM MODERN TIMES FOR EXCESSIVE ENTHUSIASM" and I stuck it on The Biscuit's rear bumper. SurfSeth420 would have been proud.

Chapter 4: The Ocean Laptop Incident (Or: When the Pacific Said "Actually, No")

There is a beach in La Jolla where the seals hang out. Not the cute museum seals. The wild, feral, screaming-at-4-AM seals who have clearly been through some things. The beach is called Children's Pool Beach, and it is simultaneously one of the most beautiful places in San Diego and one of the most aggressively smelly. You walk there and you're assaulted by two things simultaneously: the visual majesty of the California coastline and the olfactory assault of a hundred seals who have not heard of deodorant. It is a full sensory experience. It is the only place I have ever been where I had to physically choose which sense to prioritize.

I was working from this beach one Tuesday. I want to emphasize that: I was WORKING. From the BEACH. In SAN DIEGO. This is the part of the van life fantasy that Instagram does not lie about, because it is genuinely incredible. I had my laptop on a camping towel, the Pacific Ocean to my left, a sealed crack in my heart to my right, and a burrito bowl from a place called "Taco Stand" (the irony of which was not lost on me) between my knees. I was on a video call. A product demo call. I was showing a potential client how our Instagram optimization app worked and they were nodding and saying "mhm" and I was feeling like the most competent and free-spirited professional who ever lived.

And then a pelican landed on my laptop.

I want to be very clear about what happened next. I did not drop my laptop. The pelican landed on it. The pelican's intention is unclear. The pelican may have been trying to assert dominance. The pelican may have thought my laptop was a particularly flat fish. The pelican may have been having a spiritual experience and needed a surface upon which to reflect. I will never know. What I know is that my laptop, a MacBook Pro that I had financed over 24 months because I believed in the dream of being a professional, went into the ocean. Not dramatically. Not in slow motion. It just... went in. Quietly. As if it had planned it all along.

I screamed. I don't know what I screamed. It might have been a word. It might have been a seagull impression. The pelican looked at me, unimpressed, and flew away. My client was still on the call. I could see their face frozen on the screen, which was now showing me what water damage looks like from the inside of a port. They said, "Derek? Derek, are you there? Derek, your video just went... aquatic."

I dove into the ocean. I dove into the Pacific Ocean fully clothed, in work casual, to retrieve my laptop from a depth of approximately two and a half feet of water and three inches of sand. I looked like a man who had made a series of choices that were about to catch up with him. Which, to be fair, was accurate. I found the laptop. I held it up triumphantly, like a recovered treasure from an ancient shipwreck, and the screen was still on, and the client was still there, and they said, "Derek... is that seawater?" and I said, "It's ocean water, Kevin, there's a difference," and they said, "I think we need to reschedule," and I said, "Yeah, Kevin, I think we do."

I put the laptop in a bag of rice. This is the advice everyone gives, and it is correct advice, but what they don't tell you is that the rice has to be UNCOOKED rice, and you need to leave it in there for 72 hours, and during those 72 hours you cannot work, because you have no laptop, because your laptop is in a bag of rice, because a pelican made a decision about your life without consulting you. I sat in The Biscuit for three days, staring at a bag of rice that contained my entire professional identity, eating peanut butter from the jar because I couldn't afford to go out to eat because my laptop was dead and replacing it was not in the budget because I had spent all my money on a VAN.

On day four, I took the laptop out. I pressed the power button. It made a sound — a grinding, coughing, clearly-in-crisis sound — and the screen flickered on. The Apple logo appeared. It looked like it had been through something. It looked like it had been through a war. In a sense, it had been through a war: the war of me and my decisions. The laptop lived. It lived, and it worked, and to this day it has a slightly warped screen that shows colors wrong, especially blues and greens, which means every photo I look at now looks vaguely like a 1970s film filter was applied by a ghost. I told people it was an artistic choice. It was not an artistic choice. But the story of the pelican was so good that I couldn't bring myself to fix it. Some things you just have to let become part of your mythology.

Chapter 5: The Startup That Disrupted My Sleep Schedule (And Also, Allegedly, the Influencer Industry)

I was hired by a startup. The startup was called "Postful." Yes, like "plateful," but for posts. The name was the brainchild of a 26-year-old CEO named Bryce who had never held a job that wasn't his own company, and who had a standing desk that was also a treadmill, and who genuinely believed that he was going to be the next coming of Steve Jobs even though the only thing he had in common with Steve Jobs was that he also wore black turtlenecks, except Bryce's were from Everlane and cost $148 and Jobs' were from a Japanese department store and cost $4, and honestly that $4 turtleneck probably had more soul than Bryce's entire Series A pitch deck.

Postful's pitch was simple: an app that told influencers the optimal time to post on Instagram, based on an algorithm that analyzed their specific audience's engagement patterns, cross-referenced with lunar cycles, astrological transits, and the price of Bitcoin, because Bryce had read somewhere that the price of Bitcoin correlated with Instagram engagement and he had never let go of an idea since he let go of the one where he thought he could make money selling artisanal toast boards on Etsy. The app worked. Kind of. About 60% of the time it told you the right window, and 40% of the time it told you to post during a solar eclipse, which the algorithm thought was "optimal" because engagement was technically higher during solar eclipses, probably because everyone was inside looking at their phones instead of watching the actual eclipse. The irony of this was not lost on me. I was using an app to optimize my Instagram posting schedule while working from a van and eating ramen out of a sink. I was a walking case study in the attention economy.

I was their 12th employee. "Twelve" is a generous term for what I was, which was more like "the person who answered the support emails and also did the data entry and also made coffee runs and also occasionally was asked to step into a Zoom call and pretend to be the head of data science, which I was not, but no one at the company knew what data science actually was so I was effectively indistinguishable from the real thing." I wore a lot of hats. Most of them were metaphorical. One of them was an actual beanie, because it was cold in The Biscuit at 7 AM and I had not yet invested in a space heater, because investing in a space heater felt like admitting this was permanent, and I was not ready to admit that.

Bryce called me at 11 PM one night to tell me that we'd just closed a deal with a macro-influencer. 800,000 followers. Her name was BrittanyBakes and she made elaborate cakes that looked like anime characters. Her most popular video was a cake shaped like a realistic dump truck that was filled with candy and she smashed it with a sledgehammer and cried. I watched the video seven times. I respected the craft. Bryce was convinced this was going to change everything. "Derek," he said, and his voice had the intensity of a man who had just discovered that the thing he'd been believing in was actually real, "Derek, if BrittanyBakes uses Postful, we're not just an app anymore. We're a movement."

"We're an app that tells people when to post pictures of food," I said.

"Derek, I need you to believe in this," Bryce said.

"I do believe in this," I lied.

I did not believe in this. But I showed up every day at the Chula Vista Costco parking lot at 8 AM, and I opened my laptop, and I answered support emails from influencers who were genuinely distressed that the optimal posting time for their "what I eat in a day" videos had shifted by 23 minutes, and I did it with the energy of a man who had found his calling, which I had not, but the health insurance was pretty good, considering.

Chapter 6: The Seagull Incident (A Sequel, Not a Repeat)

I promised you a seagull affair and I am a man of my word. The seagull's name was Gerald. I did not name Gerald. Gerald named himself, or rather, Gerald communicated to me through a series of increasingly aggressive gestures that his name was Gerald, and I respected that, because Gerald had a presence. Gerald was not a small seagull. Gerald was a large seagull. Gerald was the kind of seagull that other seagulls probably looked up to, if seagulls had that kind of social structure, which, after spending three months in close proximity to them, I believe they absolutely do. There is a seagull hierarchy and Gerald was near the top of it.

Gerald found me at the parking lot of the RV Park in Coronado where I had moved my operations after the Costco incident. The Costco incident was never fully explained, but it involved a misinterpreted "overnight parking policy" and a very polite note from a security guard that said "Hey brother, love the van, but you gotta move" in the friendliest possible tone. The Coronado RV Park was $45 a night, which was more than I wanted to spend, but it had showers and laundry and a dog park where I did not have a dog but went anyway because there were dogs there and dogs are better than most people.

Gerald showed up on day two. He landed on The Biscuit's hood with the authority of a man returning to his ancestral home. He looked at me through the windshield. I looked at him through the windshield. We had a moment. It was not a tender moment. It was the kind of moment that happens between two apex predators who are both trying to figure out who is going to back down first. I blinked. Gerald did not blink. Gerald had clearly done this before. Gerald had stared down more intimidating opponents than a guy in a Sprinter with lower back problems and a $39.95 camping table. I conceded. I gave Gerald the rest of my breakfast burrito. Gerald ate it, looked at me once more to say "I accept this tribute, but know that I am not your friend," and flew away. I was HONORED. I had been deemed WORTHY by a seagull. This was the van life peak. It could only go downhill from here.

Chapter 7: The One Where Derek Has to Explain What He Does at Parties (And He Is Not Great At It)

"So what do you do?" she asked. She was at a rooftop party in Bay Park. I had been invited by a friend of a friend who had heard about "the van guy" and wanted to meet me, which I found flattering and also a little reductive, because I was more than the van, I was a PERSON with SKILLS and a CAREER TRAJECTORY, or at least I was pretending to be one. Her name was Amanda. She was a marine biologist. She studied octopus intelligence, which, if you think about it, is the most interesting possible thing to study, because octopuses are basically aliens who decided to give this planet a try and then got a bad review and left. Amanda was wearing a t-shirt that said "VErylarge \m/" in reference to the Very Large Array, which I only knew because my friend had told me about it, and I had forgotten until that exact moment, and now I was scrambling to make it seem like I had always known about radio telescope arrays and was not simply a man whose entire knowledge of astronomy came from watching "Contact" on a loop in The Biscuit at 1 AM.

"I work in tech," I said. This is a terrible answer. I knew it was a terrible answer even as it left my mouth. "Tech" is the answer you give when you don't want to explain what you actually do, which is often, because explaining what I actually did often led to conversations that made me want to lay face-down on the roof and accept that this was my life and I had made choices.

"What kind of tech?" Amanda asked, with the polite persistence of someone who actually wanted to know.

"Social media. It's a SaaS. We use machine learning to optimize posting schedules for influencers." I said it as fast as possible so that maybe she'd miss some of the words and I'd only have to explain half of them.

"Like... a bot?" she asked.

"Not a bot. An algorithm. There's a difference." I was lying. I was not sure there was a difference, or rather, I was sure the difference was mostly marketing. "We analyze engagement patterns."

"Oh," she said, nodding. "Like the time I posted a picture of my sister's octopus at the aquarium and it got way more likes at 2 AM than it ever would have at noon?"

"Exactly like that," I said, and I felt a little spark of something, because she had just described our exact target user behavior, probably without knowing it, and I thought, for one of the only times in my tenure at Postful, maybe this was a real product with real value, and not just Bryce's delusion with a feature list.

"What do you do?" she asked.

"I live in a van," I said, because I had decided honesty was the policy even when it made me sound like a cautionary tale.

"Like, full time?"

"Full time. The van, the beach, the parking lot. It's the full ecosystem."

"That sounds..." she paused, choosing her words carefully, "...like you have a very specific relationship with freedom."

"I have a very specific relationship with a lack of health insurance, but freedom is adjacent," I said.

She laughed. Not a polite laugh. A real laugh. The kind of laugh that makes you feel like you've won something small and unimportant but also deeply meaningful. "I like you, Derek from the van," she said.

"Derek from the van," I said. "It's a title. I'm growing into it."

Chapter 8: The Great Hygiene Chapter (Or: The Day I Discovered the Beach Shower)

I want to be honest with you about something that the van life Instagram accounts do not show you. They do not show you the hygiene aspect. They show you the sunset. They show you the coffee in the morning with the golden light and the ocean in the background and the perfectly imperfect ceramic mug. They do not show you the time you had to shower at the beach using a garden hose that was the temperature of regret and that was the GOOD option. They do not show you the morning you woke up and realized you had been wearing the same hoodie for six days and you only knew it was six days because you'd been counting, not because it looked different, but because it had achieved a certain structural integrity that transcended mere fabric.

I bathed at the beach. There is a shower at the foot of the stairs at Coronado Beach, and it is fed by the sun, and it is approximately the temperature of a hug from a reptile, but it is water, and it is technically warm if you count "lukewarm at the very end of August" as warm. I had a system. I would go at 6 AM, when no one was around, and I would stand under the shower and I would let the cold California morning water wash over me and I would think about my choices. Not in a regretful way. In a reflective way. There is a difference. Reflection is: "I am doing something a little unusual and here is why." Regret is: "I have made a terrible mistake and I should call my mother." I was reflecting. Mostly.

I also used baby wipes. A lot of baby wipes. Costco sells a 576-pack of sensitive skin baby wipes and I went through them at a rate that suggested I was either a new parent or a man in a van who had accepted certain tradeoffs. The baby wipes were the gray market of hygiene. They made you feel clean enough to pass a social distance inspection, but they did not fool a close encounter. I knew this. I accepted this. I became a person who was always slightly but not entirely fresh. A person at 73% cleanliness capacity. A person who had made peace with being a person who smelled a little like a gym sock that had been near the ocean. This is who I was now. Derek from the Van. 73% fresh.

Chapter 9: The All-Nighter at 7-Eleven (Or: The Best and Worst Night of My Life)

There was a 7-Eleven in National City that was open 24 hours and that had a seating area with four plastic chairs and free wifi that actually worked, unlike the wifi at Modern Times, which, as you will recall, had basically given up on the concept of data transfer as a noble but ultimately futile endeavor. The 7-Eleven was my office on nights when The Biscuit's battery was dying and I needed to charge my laptop and also when I needed to be near human beings who were not seals or seagulls or the various delivery drivers who had become my de facto social circle because they were the only people who regularly came to my parking spot.

I was on a deadline. Bryce had announced at 9 PM that we needed to ship a "critical feature update" by 6 AM, and the feature update was basically a button that did the same thing the old button did but was in a different color because a beta tester had said "I feel like the button could be more optimistic" and Bryce had taken this note with the seriousness of a man receiving prophecy. I was the data science team. I was also the QA team. I was also, by some miracle of organizational inefficiency, the product manager, which meant I had to write the ticket for the button I was also going to test. I was wearing every hat. I was wearing the beanie. I was wearing all of it.

I bought a Big Gulp at midnight. I bought two. I bought a taquito that I microwaved myself because I respected the process. I bought a second taquito because the first one had been transcendent and I wanted to see if the magic was repeatable, and it was not, but the second taquito still hit the spot in the way that 2 AM gas station food always hits the spot, which is to say, it was doing a job and the job was keeping me alive. The 7-Eleven clerk was a man named Eduardo who was probably in his 60s and who had the energy of someone who had seen everything and was no longer surprised by any of it. I respected Eduardo deeply. Eduardo had seen people at 2 AM doing things that I could not even imagine, and I was doing something pretty weird, sitting in a gas station eating a taquito and arguing in a Slack channel about button color psychology.

At 3 AM, the wifi cut out. Just for 20 minutes. In that 20 minutes, I had a full existential crisis, standing in front of the refrigerator section, staring at a Tub文明建设 sign that someone had taped to the fridge, and I thought: what am I doing? What am I actually doing? I am arguing about button colors in a 7-Eleven at 3 AM for a startup that may or may not exist in two years, living in a van, eating gas station food, banned from a coffee shop, with a pelican's victim living in my laptop. Is this freedom? Is this what freedom looks like when you strip away all the things that were weighing you down? Or is this just a different set of weights?

The wifi came back. The crisis passed. I went back to my plastic chair. I changed the button from #3B82F6 to #10B981, which is green instead of blue, which is optimistic instead of corporate, which is the difference between a startup that cares and a startup that has given up on the concept of caring. I pushed the commit at 5:47 AM. I finished with 13 minutes to spare. Eduardo gave me a nod on the way out. I nodded back. We understood each other. We were both still standing. We were both still here.

Chapter 10: The Worst Meeting I Ever Had (And the One After It)

Bryce called an all-hands at 10 AM on a Wednesday. This was unusual, because Postful's "all-hands" consisted of 12 people who were all at various levels of attendance — some on video, some on phone, one guy named Marco who had been on two all-hands calls in a row and then never on any call again and no one had asked about it because asking about it would mean acknowledging that the startup had a retention problem that went beyond normal tech industry churn. The all-hands was about "direction." Bryce had that look. The look of a man who had been told by his investors that he needed to "recalibrate expectations" and who had decided that the way to do that was to call a meeting where he would use the word "pivot" at least seven times.

"We are pivoting," Bryce said. He said it the way people say "we're having pot roast for dinner" — like it was a normal decision that had been made through a rational process and not like he had just pulled the word out of the air because his Series A had crumbled and the only term sheet he had left was from a VC firm that had also backed a company that made edible water bottles and that company had just gone under. "We are pivoting from influencers to something we believe in more deeply. We believe in LOCAL BUSINESSES. We believe in the corner store. We believe in the bodega."

"We are pivoting from BrittanyBakes to the corner bodega," I said, not as a question.

"Derek, I need you on board with this."

"I'm on board with whatever keeps the health insurance going," I said, which was honest and also the most real thing anyone said in the entire meeting, and everyone on the call went very quiet, and I realized that the health insurance thing was probably not something I was supposed to say out loud in an all-hands, but it was also what everyone was thinking, and someone had to say it, and that someone was going to be me, the guy in the van, the guy who had nothing left to lose because he had already lost most of his possessions in a series of decisions that had culminated in living in a 2004 Dodge Sprinter named after a breakfast food.

The pivot did not work. Obviously. You cannot pivot from influencers to local businesses using the same algorithm that tracks lunar cycles and Bitcoin prices. The algorithm did not care about the corner bodega. The algorithm cared about engagement. The corner bodega did not have an Instagram. The corner bodega had a handwritten sign in the window that said "WE DON'T TAKE CARDS WE ONLY DO CASH OR VENMO" and that was the entire digital footprint of the corner bodega and it was beautiful and it was not compatible with Postful's vision and we all knew it and we shipped the pivot anyway because shipping was what we did, not because it was right, but because standing still felt worse.

Chapter 11: The Day the Van Broke Down (Or: The Biscuit Strikes Back)

The Biscuit broke down on a Tuesday. Of course it was a Tuesday. Tuesdays are the day of the week that has never once in the history of the universe done anything right. Something always goes wrong on a Tuesday. The universe saves its petty inconveniences for Tuesdays like a divine scheduler who thought it would be funny.

I was on the 163 northbound, heading to a meeting in La Jolla, which I was attending via my phone because the meeting was about "team culture" and I had decided that "team culture" was something that happened to other teams and that my contribution to team culture was showing up in the parking lot and being a body on the Zoom call. The Biscuit made a sound. Not the usual sound — not the check engine light symphony that had been my constant companion since SurfSeth420 and I had completed our Craigslist transaction. This was a new sound. A grinding sound. A sound that said, "Derek, I have been holding it together through sheer force of will and I am done."

The Biscuit pulled to the side of the road. Not dramatically. Not with a cloud of smoke or a hiss of steam or any of the cinematic elements that would have made this a great story. She just... stopped. Engine off. Hazards on. Derek, stranded on the side of the 163, at 9:17 AM, in the middle of a lane merge, with a meeting that was starting in 13 minutes and a van that was not going anywhere under its own power.

I called a tow truck. The tow truck took 47 minutes to arrive, which felt like 47 years, and in that time I sat on the side of the road and ate the emergency granola bars I kept in the van for exactly this kind of situation and I thought about my life. Not in a deep, philosophical way. In a practical, slightly panicked way. The van was dead. The Biscuit had finally said uncle. And I was on the side of a highway in San Diego with a broken van and a laptop that still had water damage and a job at a startup that was pivoting into oblivion. This was either a low point or a turning point and I genuinely could not tell which.

The tow truck driver was a woman named Rosa who had been driving tow trucks in San Diego for 22 years and who had seen everything. I told her the van was a 2004 Dodge Sprinter and she said, "Oh, the Biscuit?" and I said, "How do you know it's called the Biscuit?" and she said, "Man, I read the bumper sticker. It says 'BISCUIT LIVES MATTER' on the back." It did. I had forgotten I had put that there. I had put it there ironically but it had since transcended irony and become a genuine statement of belief in The Biscuit as a living entity with rights and feelings. Rosa gave me a card for a mechanic in El Cajon who she said "wouldn't rip me off" and who she also said "might actually be a wizard, because he has fixed things on my truck that I thought were unfixable and he did it for $200 and I still don't understand how."

The mechanic's name was Doc. He was not a doctor of anything except automotive repair, which, honestly, is a more useful doctorate than most. Doc looked at The Biscuit and said, "Your transmission is crying." I said, "The transmission is crying?" and he said, "Yeah, it makes a sound like crying. It's been overworked. You been doing a lot of stop-and-go in this thing?" and I said, "I live in it," and he said, "Oh, THAT explains it," and he said it in a way that suggested that he saw this regularly, that there were many Dereks out there in the world, living in vans, pushing their transmissions to the breaking point, and that he had seen enough of us to have a professional opinion. Doc fixed The Biscuit for $680, which was more than I wanted to spend but less than a new transmission, and when I picked her up she ran better than she had in months, as if Doc had given her a new lease on life, and maybe he had.

---

Chapter 12: The Final Chapter (Or: What Derek Learned, Allegedly)

I still live in The Biscuit. It has now been seven months. The check engine light is back on — it never really left — and I have decided that it is not a warning light but a companion. A little friend that lives on my dashboard and says "hey, remember you're doing something unusual." The pelican still haunts my laptop screen every time I look at a photo of the ocean. Gerald the seagull still appears to me in dreams. I've been banned from two more coffee shops since Modern Times, though the second one was technically a library and the banning was more of a "please stop brought a live frog inside" situation that I maintain was blown out of proportion.

Amanda and I went on one date. It was lovely. She showed me her lab and the octopuses and I showed her The Biscuit and the octopuses were objectively more interesting but Amanda was a close second. We didn't go on a second date, not because it went badly, but because she got a research fellowship in Hawaii and I got a new job at a different startup that makes software for accounting firms and I think we both knew that the van life phase of my life was a chapter, not the whole book, and hers was a different book entirely, one that involved labs and grant applications and indoor plumbing on a regular basis.

The new job pays better. The new job has an office. The new job has a desk that is a desk and not a laptop velcroed to a piece of plywood with bungee cords. I go into the office three days a week and I sit at my desk and I do accounting software and it is fine. It is aggressively fine. It is the most fine job anyone has ever had. And I am grateful for it in a way that I could not have been grateful before, because before I did not know what the alternative looked like when it was cold and dark and your van wouldn't start and you were eating a taquito at 2 AM in a 7-Eleven in National City and your boss was pivoting the company into a market segment that did not exist.

On the weekends, I still sleep in The Biscuit. Not because I have to. Because I want to. Because there is something about the way the Pacific sounds when you're falling asleep 200 feet from it, in a van named after a bread product, with a check engine light glowing softly on the dashboard like a tiny beacon of "yes, this is unusual, and that's okay." I am Derek from the Van. I will always be Derek from the Van. Even when I am Derek from the Apartment with the Desk That Is A Desk. The van was a chapter that changed the book. And this is the epilogue. The epilogue is me, eating ramen out of the van's sink at 2 AM, watching the stars, knowing that I did it, that I actually did it, that I lived the weird life and I survived it and I came out the other side with a story that I will be telling people for the rest of my life.

And that story is this.

This is the story of how a guy named Derek bought a van named The Biscuit and drove it to San Diego and worked remotely and got banned from coffee shops and was dive-bombed by a pelican and befriended a seagull named Gerald and pivoted with a startup and ate taquitos at midnight and showered at a beach at 6 AM and lived to tell about it.

This is the story of the time I was too free and didn't know what to do with it and figured it out anyway.

This is the story.

The end.

(Except it is never really the end. It's just the end of this chapter. The next chapter is the one you write after you close the laptop and look up at the stars and realize you are alive and in San Diego and that's enough. That's always enough.)
